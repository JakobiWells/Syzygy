## The Principles

Everything in this guide runs on five ideas. If something doesn't make sense later, come back here.

### Less is best

Less is cheaper. Less is easier to clean. Less is easier to cook. When in doubt, do less.

### Keep it simple, stupid (KISS)

Complexity is the enemy of consistency. If a process is complicated enough that you'll skip it when you're tired, it's too complicated. Simple systems get used. Elaborate ones collect dust.

### Quality over quantity

Four mismatched sets of silverware when one good set would do. A cabinet full of spices you bought once and never touched. The goal isn't a full kitchen, it's the right kitchen.

### Abstraction

The best processes, tools, and recipes work across situations. A good knife works on everything. A good base recipe adapts to whatever you have. We want things that generalize, not things that solve one narrow problem.

### Accessibility

This should work for anyone. Regardless of income, diet, age, culture, or cooking experience. No exotic ingredients, no expensive equipment required upfront.

## The Staples

Nearly every restaurant on the planet maintains a standing inventory — foods, spices, tools, and equipment that are always stocked. The core of this system borrows from that idea.

Staples are items you always have. Not "usually have." Always. If you're out of a staple, it goes on the list immediately, not when you get around to it.

Here's the shift I want you to make.

Most home cooks figure out what they need. Experienced kitchen managers figure out what they don't have. The difference sounds small. It isn't.

While you're building a meal plan, finding recipes, and writing a grocery list from scratch every week, a restaurant manager walks the kitchen, checks inventory, and restocks what's missing. Same result, a fraction of the effort.

That's the model. You're not planning meals around what you want. You're maintaining a system and cooking from what's there.

With this model in mind, I want you to imagine that you are building a kitchen from scratch as we move forward.

I believe the easiest staple to start with is spices. Spices are cheap, virtually never go bad, easy to store, and, of course, essential to any dish. Given this, having an organized and abundant spice selection is important.

Nothing is more frustrating than wanting to try a new recipe or experiment with a new cuisine and not having that spice. Our new kitchen will never have this problem.
